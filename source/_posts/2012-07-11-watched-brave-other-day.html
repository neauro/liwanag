The movie on the whole is beautiful, but the characters and story felt shallow and unfocused. Soon after the start of the movie, Merida describes her family and lifestyle, from the fact that her mother is controlling to the existence of her three younger brothers -- a huge contrast from introductory sequences a la WALL-E and Up, where you are given the chance to watch characters interact, learn what they do and what they love without anyone explaining it to you like a textbook.

Throughout the movie there were little actions that I wish had had more background or more follow-through, more meaning in general.

For example, when her mother gave her that necklace -- how important was it? Wouldn't it have felt so much more painful to watch Merida give that necklace away to the witch if you had known anything about it? If it was something her mother had inherited, something that stood for all the tradition and love that her mother was trying to give to her, which Merida then selfishly threw away?

And, why is it so important for Merida to be introduced as an amazing archer -- what purpose did her archery ever serve to the story? There's the competition scene, but her shooting in that case didn't make any difference to the fact she was still going to be married off. For the rest of the movie, whenever she shoots an arrow (like at that Big Evil Magic Bear) it doesn't do anything. Contrast to Katniss in The Hunger Games, whose proficient archery is used at all times to express her character: it's used in her illicit yet altruistic hunting activity, it's what she uses to defy the Capitol during her evaluation, it's what she uses to explode her enemies' supplies before she's ready to actually kill them, and it's what she uses when she does kill her first human being. In fact, at the end of Brave you saw Merida showing off more of her sword skills when she prevents her father from attacking her mother. So, why stress her archery?

The movie also worked off some assumptions of marriage that I wish had been explained. Like -- why is Merida so immediately opposed to marriage? Why does marriage even need to happen in this story, other than the fact that it's "The Tradition"? At some point Merida's mother starts lecturing off this story about how Tradition is Important, but it still doesn't have any tie-in to why Merida has to be married, or why this is such an oppressive thing. I mean, her mother is doing okay, right?  Her mother is shown to wield an incredible amount of respect among all the other men in the movie, walking among their brawls without a scratch, pacifying them with ear pinches.

And the way this "I don't want to be married!" conflict ended made me cringe. Essentially, Merida confronts the other clan leaders and their sons and, with the blessing of her (pantomiming, bear-shaped) mother, declares "It's time for us to break away from tradition!"

And the men go, "Hmm, okay!"

It was just so....aww, man. Because, assuming marriage is an institution of oppression towards women (which it's treated as during this movie), it's dissolved way too easily -- essentially by women (Merida, and her mother) deciding for themselves that they no longer want to be held beneath it. It reeks of the idea that people choose whether or not to be a victim -- as if this whole time, Merida and her mother had just been "oppressing themselves." Brave is a fictional children's movie, but the idea that "People can just get whatever they want if they just ask for it" is one that's very well-believed, and in the end I would have preferred to see Merida really fight to "change her fate," as the move's tagline stated, instead of "discover that the true power to succeed was within her all along," which is blatantly American Dream-y.

Really fight someone else other than her mother, I mean, who was I guess is the only person in this movie not progressive enough to be okay with Merida not marrying.

Even if you don't want to think about it from a social justice-y standpoint, it's still mind-boggling why the problem Merida had in this movie was marriage. Merida's mother wanted marriage for the purposes of "keeping peace" between the clans; but the clan-leaders and their sons clearly didn't want marriage badly enough to argue when Merida proposed an alternative.

The last thing I want to mention: what was up with that magical warrior bear? I guess it was chasing Merida at the very beginning of the movie, and at the end, surrounded by people, was only interested in mauling her?   The way that he dissolved into blue at the end seemed to imply that he had some relation to the will-o-the-wisps, but I don't get how Merida was important to him at all.

That all being said, I guess I'm still looking forward to Pixar's next movie.
